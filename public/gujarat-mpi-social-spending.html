Rich state, poor outcomes: why Gujarat's growth hasn't bought down poverty

Gujarat sits among India's wealthiest large states by per-capita income, yet its multidimensional poverty rate is nearly identical to West Bengal's — a state with half its income. The gap traces back to one number: how little of Gujarat's budget goes to health, education and welfare.

01 — The paradox

Twice the income, almost the same poverty rate

By 2021-22, Gujarat's per-capita income (₹2.5 lakh) was roughly double West Bengal's (₹1.24 lakh). On NITI Aayog's National Multidimensional Poverty Index — which measures deprivation in health, education and living standards, not just income — the two states are nearly indistinguishable.

These three indicators are widely considered the most directly addressable through targeted infrastructure spending (toilets, piped water, household electrification), as opposed to nutrition or education outcomes which take longer to shift.

Gujarat's MPI is still substantially a nutrition story: 38.09% of Gujarat's total population was nutrition-deprived in 2019-21 — the single largest deprivation of any indicator, and only modestly below the national average of 31.52%. But Gujarat also still has real gaps in basics that money can fix faster: 26.05% of Gujaratis lacked adequate sanitation and 34.74% still cooked with solid fuel in 2019-21 — both well above what a state with Gujarat's per-capita income would suggest, and both squarely the kind of indicator that sustained capital and revenue spending moves quickly, as Bihar's even larger declines on the same two indicators (-22.7pp sanitation, -19.6pp cooking fuel) show is achievable even from a much lower base.

02 — The spending gap, eleven years running

Gujarat hasn't ranked above last place once since 2011-12

This isn't a single bad year. Computing social sector expenditure as a share of GSDP for every major state, every year from 2011-12 to 2021-22 — using RBI's own expenditure and GSDP series — Gujarat finishes dead last, or second-to-last, in every single year on record.

In 2021-22, Gujarat spent 4.30% of GSDP on social services — the lowest of any major state in this dataset. Maharashtra (5.22%) and Tamil Nadu (5.55%) are the only other big, high-income states anywhere close to Gujarat's range; the difference is that Gujarat has been at or near the floor in every year since 2011-12, not just the most recent one. Bihar, by contrast, spent more than 3.5 times Gujarat's share of GSDP (15.61%) in the same year.

03 — A persistent gap, not a one-off

The gap between Gujarat and its peers has been remarkably stable

Gujarat's social sector expenditure ratio moved in a narrow band — between 4.3% and 5.25% of GSDP — across the entire eleven-year window. It never closed in on Maharashtra or Karnataka for long, and it never approached the all-state norm of 7-10% that mid-income states like Madhya Pradesh, Odisha and Rajasthan settled into after 2014-15.

04 — Reading the pattern

Income explains Gujarat's growth story. It doesn't explain its poverty numbers.

Three things sit alongside each other in this data. First, Gujarat's MPI headcount ratio in 2019-21 was statistically close to West Bengal's despite a roughly 2x income gap — a comparison NITI Aayog's own report implicitly invites by publishing both states' numbers side by side. Second, computing social sector expenditure as a share of GSDP directly from RBI's own primary tables shows Gujarat at or near the bottom of every major state in every one of the eleven years from 2011-12 to 2021-22 — not a single year's anomaly, but a sustained pattern across two state governments' worth of budgets. Third, the gap to peers with comparable income levels — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu — never closed; if anything it widened slightly in Gujarat's most recent years on record.

None of this is proof of a single causal chain — GSDP composition, urbanization rates, and historical baselines differ across states, and a state spending less per rupee of GSDP isn't automatically spending less per capita if its GSDP is larger. But the consistency of the pattern across eleven years — high income, a social spending share stuck near 4-5% of GSDP, and a poverty-reduction pace among the slowest of any major state — is hard to read as coincidence, and it matches what Gujarat's own health and education indices have separately shown: a state whose human-development outcomes have not kept pace with its income growth.
